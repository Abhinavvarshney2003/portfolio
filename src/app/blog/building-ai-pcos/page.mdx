# How I Built an AI Model for Early PCOS Detection

May 15, 2026 • AI / ML • 5 min read

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) affects millions of women worldwide, yet early and accurate diagnosis remains a significant medical challenge. The primary diagnostic tool—ultrasound imaging—often yields results that are highly subjective, varying greatly depending on the sonographer's experience and the quality of the equipment. 

In my latest research project, I set out to determine if we could build a more objective, consistent baseline for diagnosis using computer vision and deep learning. Here's a technical breakdown of how I approached the problem.

## The Architecture

The pipeline consists of three distinct phases:

1. **Synthetic Data Generation (GANs)**
2. **Semantic Segmentation (U-Net)**
3. **Object Detection (YOLOv8)**

### Phase 1: Overcoming the Data Drought

Medical datasets are notoriously difficult to acquire due to privacy concerns. To solve the problem of class imbalance and scarce training data, I utilized Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). 

By training a custom GAN on a small verified dataset of PCOS-positive ultrasounds, I was able to synthesize thousands of realistic variations.

### Phase 2: Segmentation with U-Net

Once I had a robust dataset, the next step was to isolate the region of interest. Ultrasound images contain a lot of noise (speckle). Feeding the entire image into a classifier would likely result in the model learning irrelevant artifacts.

I deployed a U-Net architecture to create a segmentation mask that specifically isolates the ovarian boundaries.

### Phase 3: YOLOv8 for Cyst Detection

With the ovary isolated, I used YOLOv8 to detect and count the number of follicles (cysts). A follicle count greater than 12 in a single ovary is a strong clinical indicator of PCOS. 

YOLOv8 proved exceptionally fast, allowing the entire pipeline to run in near real-time—a crucial requirement if this system were ever deployed in a live clinical setting alongside an ultrasound machine.

## Challenges and Learnings

The biggest challenge was **interpretability**. Doctors cannot trust a "black box" model that simply outputs a probability score. To solve this, I integrated Grad-CAM (Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping). 

Grad-CAM generates a heatmap overlay on the original ultrasound, showing the doctor exactly *where* the model is looking when it makes its prediction. If the model focuses on the cysts, the doctor can trust the result. If it focuses on a random shadow, the doctor knows to disregard it.

## Conclusion

AI will not replace doctors, but doctors who use AI will replace those who don't. By providing a consistent, objective second opinion, models like this can significantly reduce diagnostic variance in conditions like PCOS.
